Lecture 11: Ekman Layers and Ekman spiral
 
In response to wind stress and the Coriolis effect, wind-driven surface currents flow at 45 degrees to the right (northern hemisphere) of the wind. Below the surface, the frictional currents reduce with depth at an e-folding scale given by the Ekman depth, about 100 m. As they reduce, the balance between friction and Coriolis turns the current farther to the right, forming an Ekman spiral. The average motion of this spiral of currents forming the Ekman layer is at 90 degrees to the right of the wind.
 
Convergences and divergences in Ekman layer transports resulting from the large-scale wind field act to pile up water, or remove water, in different regions of the ocean. For a subtropical gyre the wind field is such that Ekman transports pile up water into the centre of the gyre: there is a horizontal convergence of the flow within the Ekman layer. Such a convergence of water must lead to a downward transport of waters out of the Ekman layer and into the interior ocean in the centre of the gyre - this is called Ekman pumping. Similarly, in regions of divergent Ekman transports there is an upward transport of waters into the Ekman layer: Ekman suction.
