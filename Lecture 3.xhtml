1. Physical-chemical properties of the water molecule
The water molecule, chemical formula H2O, is formed by two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to an oxygen atom. The molecule is not linear, with the hydrogen atoms forming an angle of ~104° with respect to the oxygen atom. This bent geometry, associated with the fact that the oxygen atom is more electronegative than the hydrogen ones, results in a polar moment, i.e., the water molecule has a separation of positive and negative electrical charges. This property imbues the water with some very important characteristics:
  • High dielectric constant: water and can hold/store large electrical charge. Hence, it is a good polar solvent and can store a large number of ions in solution, such as Na+ and Cl−;
  • Linking together of molecules into polymer chains: the large polar momentum of water molecules and its geometry allows them to form up to four strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds. This characteristic leads to:
    • Large heat capacity: when water is heated, much of the energy goes into breaking the polymer chains, rather than increasing temperature;
    • High viscosity and surface tension: at the surface, polymer chain aligns horizontally to form a ”skin” of high surface tension;
    • Maximum density of water is in its liquid state: the polymerization decrease with increasing T, and vice-versa. Between 0°C and 4°C, polymers are still getting longer but ice crystals are beginning to form, which occupy more space and act to decrease density. As number of ice crystals grows, their effect on ρ dominates until water becomes solid at a lower density;
2. Salinity
At simplest level, salinity (hereafter S) is the mass in grams (g) of dissolved material per Kg of seawater. This leads to a dimensionless quantity, but can be thought as parts per thousand. Dittmar (1884), using data from the Challenger Expedition, noticed that the ratios of the various ions in seawater are largely independent of salinity and locations. The table below shows the relative abundances of the main dissolved ions on oceanic waters:
Chloride
 50%
Sodium (NA)
 31%
Sulphate (S)
 8%
Magnesium (MG)
 4%
Calcium (Ca) / Potassium (K)
 1%

Constant ratio of ions led to measurement of salinity by titration of chloride with silver nitrate (S = 1.80655 Cl, accuracy 0.01‰).

Since 1970’s (invention of CTD), S is quantified in terms of the practical salinity unit (PSU), which relates measurements of the seawater conductivity to salinity. Conductivity depends strongly on temperature, and weakly on pressure. The relationship between conductivity and salinity has an accuracy of 0.003. This error is caused by variations in semi-conservative constituents such as SiO2, which increases salinity but do not affect conductivity.

The salty ocean is different from freshwater as it gets denser from right down to frozen point. (−2°C). Hence it is difficult to make ice in the ocean - the whole water column has to freeze.

3. Temperature
Temperature (hereafter T) is a thermodynamic measure of a fluid and relates to the activity level of the molecules and atoms. More active the molecules, the higher the temperature.
  • T is given in degrees Celsius (°C) and occasionally in Kelvin (K) for heat content calculations;
  • Its in situ measurement is made by using a platinum resistance thermometer, which consists of loosely wound, stain-free pure platinum wire where resistance varies as function of temperature.
